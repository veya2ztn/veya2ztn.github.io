Concurrence

Even for small N, we can still get many interesting understandings.

The first one is QPT–quantum phase transition~! for concurrence.

Before we start discussing, we need to get to figure out ‘what is concurrence’.

Quantum entanglement has rightly been the subject of much study in recent years as a potential resource for communication and information processing. As with other resources such as free energy and information, one would like to have a quantitative theory of entanglement giving specific rules about how it can and cannot be manipulated; indeed, such a theory has begun to be developed. The first step in building the theory has been to quantify entanglement itself.

The most famous way is called concurrence. Underline! Concurrence can only directly measure two system entanglement!

we can define any state’s concurrence by its density matrix $\rho_{AB}$
$$
\tilde{\rho} _{\text{AB}}=(\sigma _y\otimes \sigma _y) \rho _{\text{AB}} (\sigma _y\otimes \sigma _y)
$$

$$
\sigma _y=\left(
\begin{array}{cc}
0 & -i \\
i & 0 \\
\end{array}
\right)
$$

then we put $\rho_{AB}$and $\tilde{\rho}_{AB}$ together, consider the eigenvalue of matrix $\rho_{AB}\tilde{\rho}_{AB}$
$$
\lambda _1 ,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\lambda_4 =eign(\rho_{AB}\tilde{\rho}_{AB})
$$

$$
\lambda _1> \lambda_2>\lambda_3>\lambda_4
$$

and the concurrence is be defined follow
$$
C_{AB}=\max { \sqrt{\lambda _1} - \sqrt{\lambda_2}-\sqrt{\lambda_3}-\sqrt{\lambda_4} ,0 }
$$
we can prove if $\rho$ is a pure state we can easily get
$$
C_{AB}=2 \sqrt{\det \rho_A}
$$

$$
\rho_A = Tr_B \rho_{AB}
$$

you can find more detail in the reference Distributed entanglement Valerie Coffman

classical model

The principle way to solve the classical ising model is the Transfer Matrix Formalism. Use this method, we can exactly solve the ising model opposed of other method like mean field theory.

1D classical ising model

$$
H(\sigma)=-J\sum_{i=1,\ldots,L} \sigma_i \sigma_{i+1} - h \sum_i \sigma_i
$$

where J and h can be any number, since in this simplified case J is a constant representing the interaction strength between the nearest neighbors and h is the constant external magnetic field applied to lattice sites.

with the periodic boundary conditions on the spins so that $\sigma_{N+1}=\sigma_1$

we can write the partition function
$$
Z=\sum_{\sigma}e^{\beta H}
$$
let us define a matrix P with matrix elements
$$
P=\begin{bmatrix} e^{\beta(h+J)}&e^{-\beta J}\ e^{-\beta J}&e^{-\beta(h-J)} \end{bmatrix}
$$

$$
P_{\sigma, \sigma’} = e^{\frac{\beta h}{2} \sigma} e^{\beta J\sigma\sigma’} e^{\frac{\beta h}{2} \sigma’}
$$

so that the partition function can be right
$$
Z=\sum_{\sigma}\left \langle \sigma_1|P|\sigma_2\right\rangle\left \langle \sigma_2|P|\sigma_3\right\rangle \cdots \left \langle\sigma_n|P|\sigma_1\right\rangle=Tr(P^N)
$$
let eigenvalues of P are $\lambda_+$ and $\lambda_-$, we can get the partition function is
$$
Z=\lambda_+^N+\lambda_-^N
$$

$$
\lambda_{\pm}=e^{\beta J}[\cosh(\beta h)\pm\sqrt{\sinh^2(\beta h )+e^{-4 \beta J}}]
$$

In the thermodynamic theory, if we get the partition function we can easily compute any thermodynamic quantity.

2D classical ising model

$$
H=\sum_{ij} (K\sigma_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j+1} +L \sigma_{i,j} \sigma_{i+1,j} )-h\sum_{ij}\sigma_{i,j}
$$

the exactly solution for 2D classical ising model is given by Onsager. His solution is just suitable for no external field (h=0)

consider $\mu_{\alpha}\equiv{\sigma_{1,\alpha},\sigma_{2,\alpha},\cdots,\sigma_{n,\alpha}}$ as the $\alpha$ row. Apply the periodic boundary conditions like $\mu_{n+1}=\mu_1$ we can let Hamiltonian be
$$
H=\sum_{j=1}^n[E(\mu_j,\mu_{j+1}+E(\mu_j))]
$$
where
$$
E(\mu_j,\mu_k)=-\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_{ij}\sigma_{ik}
$$

$$
E(\mu_j)=-J\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{ij}\sigma_{i+1,j}-h\sum_{i,j}\sigma_j
$$

we can now easily define the transfer matrix P as
$$
\left \langle \mu_j|P|\mu_k\right\rangle=e^{-\beta [E(\mu_j,\mu_k)+E(\mu_j)]}
$$
where P is a $2^n \times 2^n$ matrix and partition function is
$$
Z=Tr(P^N)
$$
how to present the eigenvalues of P is not a easy work.
If we add our limitation like $n\rightarrow \infty$ then the partition function Z
$$
Z=\sum_{i=1}^{2^n}\lambda_i^n
$$
can only rely on the max eigenvalues $\lambda_{max}$, since
$$
\lambda_{max}^n \leqslant Z\leqslant2^n\lambda_{max}^n
$$

$$
\frac{1}{n}\log\lambda_{max}\leqslant \frac{1}{n^2}\log Z \leqslant \frac{1}{n}\log\lambda_{max}+ \frac{1}{n}\log2
$$

therefore
$$
\lim_{N\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{N}\log Z =\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{n}\log \lambda_{max}
$$
where: $N=n\times n$

we can decompose the transfer matrix
$$
P=V_3\times V_2 \times V_1’
$$

$$
\left \langle \sigma_1,\sigma_2,\cdots,\sigma_n|V_1’|\sigma_1’,\sigma_2’,\cdots,\sigma_n’\right\rangle=\prod_{k=1}^n e^{\beta \epsilon \sigma_k \sigma_k’}
$$

$$
\left \langle \sigma_1,\sigma_2,\cdots,\sigma_n|V_2|\sigma_1’,\sigma_2’,\cdots,\sigma_n’\right\rangle=\delta_{s_1}^{s_1’}\cdots\delta_{s_n}^{s_n’}\prod_{k=1}^n e^{\beta \epsilon \sigma_k \sigma_{k+1}}
$$

$$
\left \langle \sigma_1,\sigma_2,\cdots,\sigma_n|V_3|\sigma_1’,\sigma_2’,\cdots,\sigma_n’\right\rangle=\delta_{s_1}^{s_1’}\cdots\delta_{s_n}^{s_n’}\prod_{k=1}^n e^{\beta h \sigma_k }
$$

using the eigen-representation
$$
V_1’=[2\sinh(2\beta\epsilon)]^{n/2}V_1
$$

$$
V_1=\prod_{i=1}^n e^{\theta X_i}
$$

$$
V_2=\prod_{i=1}^n e^{\beta \epsilon Z_i Z_{i+1}}
$$

$$
V_3=\prod_{i=1}^n e^{\beta h Z_i}
$$

satisfy $Z_{n+1}=Z_1$ , $\tanh\theta=e^{-2\beta \epsilon}$

using Grassmann algebra we can easily rewrite the $P$ when $h=0$
$$
P=[2\sinh(2\beta\epsilon)]^{n/2}V_2V_1
$$

$$
P=[2\sinh(2\beta\epsilon)]^{n/2}e^{i\Phi U \Gamma_1 \Gamma_{2n} }[\prod_{i=1}^{n-1}e^{-i\Phi\Gamma_{2i+1}\Gamma_{2i}}][\prod_{i=1}^{n}e^{-i\Phi\Gamma_{2i}\Gamma_{2i-1}}]
$$

where
$$
\Phi=\beta \epsilon,\epsilon>0,\theta=\tanh^{-1}e^{-2\Phi}
$$
where
$$
U=X_1X_2\cdots X_n
$$

$$
\Gamma_{2i-1}=X_1X_2\dots X_{i-1}Z_{i}
$$

$$
\Gamma_{2i}=X_1X_2\cdots X_{i-1}Y_i
$$

satisfy anticommutation rule $\Gamma_{\mu}\Gamma_{\nu}+\Gamma_{\nu}\Gamma_{\mu}=2\delta_{\mu\nu}$

then use the principle for vector rotation in the space for matrix we can write above form as rotation representation and easily get the eigenvalues. More delta derivation can follow the statistical mechanics textbook written by Huang, Kerson

Finally, we get the
$$
\lim_{N\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{N}\log Z =\frac{1}{2}\log(\frac{2\cosh^22\beta\epsilon}{\sinh2\beta\epsilon})+\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}d\phi\log(\frac{1+\sqrt{1-\kappa^2\sin^2\phi}}{2})
$$
where
$$
D=\cosh2\phi \coth2\phi,\kappa=\frac{2}{D}
$$

D+1 model map to D quantum model

It can be proved that D+1 classical statistic model and D quantum model has a very subtle relation. Under the transfer matrix method, the quantum model identify with classical model.

Our theory begins at that the transfer matrix P can approximate
$$
P=e^{-H \delta\tau}\approx1-H\delta\tau
$$
and quantum system partition function with imaginary time path integral
$$
Z=Tr(e^{-\beta H})\sum_x \sum_{x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_N}\left \langle x|e^{-H\delta\tau}|x_1\right\rangle\left \langle x_1|e^{-H\delta\tau}|x_2\right\rangle \cdots \left \langle x_n|e^{-H\delta\tau}|x\right\rangle
$$
where $\delta\tau$ is much smaller than all time scales of $H$ and $N \delta \tau =\beta$. And it means that the temperture T for a quantum system is mapped to the site number for a D+1 classical model.

1D to 0D

For simplicity, I consider the classical Ising chain with no magnetic ﬁeld but with on-site energies:
$$
\beta H = -K\sum_{ij}(s_is_j-1)
$$
The transfer matrix is obviously,
$$
T=\begin{bmatrix}1 &e^{-\beta K}\ e^{-\beta K}&1\end{bmatrix}=I+e^{-2K}\sigma_x
$$
and the partition function
$$
Z=tr(T^N)=(1-e^{-2K})^N+(1+e^{-2K})^N=\lambda_1^N+\lambda_2^N
$$
The correlation function is defined $ \left \langle s_i s_j\right\rangle $
$$
\left \langle s_i s_j\right\rangle=\frac{\lambda_1^{N-j+i}\lambda_2^{j-i}+\lambda_2^{N-j+i}\lambda_1^{j-i}}{\lambda_1^N+\lambda_2^N}
$$

when the $N \to \infty$ the correlation become $\left \langle s_i s_j\right\rangle=\tanh(K)^{j-i}$

the correlation length then is (for K>>1)
$$
\xi = -a \ln \tanh K \approx ae^{2K}/2
$$
for a 0D quantum Hamiltonian
$$
H=-e^{-2K}\sigma^x=\frac{\Delta}{2}\sigma^x
$$
where $\Delta=-2e^{-2K}$ is the energy gap.

we can easily get the relationship that
$$
\Delta= \frac{1}{\xi}
$$
so now the transfer matrix derive from quantum system is
$$
P=e^{-H \delta\tau}\approx1-H\delta\tau=I-e^{-2K}\sigma_x
$$

2D to 1D

2D classical question has been talked above. Now we just discuss directly. The Hamiltonian
$$
H=\sum_{ij} (K\sigma_{i,j} \sigma_{i,j+1} +J \sigma_{i,j} \sigma_{i+1,j} )=\sum_{i}L(i+1,i)
$$
we can let $\sigma(n_x) = \sigma_{x,j}$and $s(n_x)=\sigma_{x,j+1}$ then
$$
L(j+1,j)=\frac{1}{2}K\sum_{n_x}(s(n_x)-\sigma(n_x))^2+\frac{1}{2}J\sum_{n_x}(\sigma(n_x+1)\sigma(n_x)+s(n_x+1)s(n_x))
$$
For the diagonal element of the matrix $s(n_x)=\sigma(n_x)$ so that
$$
L(0flips)=+J\sum_{n_x}\sigma(n_x+1)\sigma(n_x)
$$

$$
L(1flips)=2K+\frac{1}{2}J\sum_{n_x}[(\sigma(n_x+1)\sigma(n_x)+s(n_x+1)s(n_x))]
$$

$$
L(nflips)=2nK+\frac{1}{2}J\sum_{n_x}[(\sigma(n_x+1)\sigma(n_x)+s(n_x+1)s(n_x))]
$$

the transfer matrix is defined like $T(j+1,j)=e^{-L(j+1,j)}$
$$
T(0flips)=\exp(-J\sum_{n_x}\sigma(n_x+1)\sigma(n_x))\approx1-\tau H|_{0flips}
$$

$$
T(1flips)\approx-\tau H|_{1flips};T(nflips)\approx-\tau H|_{nflips}
$$

if we identity $\tau=e^{-2K}$and $J=\lambda2^{2K}$ to obtain a condition that is consistent with $\tau\to0$.Therefore $\tau \to 0$,$K\to\infty$

you will find that it is same as a transverse quantum ising model
$$
H=-\sum_m \sigma_m^x-\lambda\sum_m\sigma_{m+1}^z \sigma_m^z
$$
