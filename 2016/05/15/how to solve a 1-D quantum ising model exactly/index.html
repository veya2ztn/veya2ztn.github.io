how to solve a 1-D quantum ising model exactly

Posted on 2016-05-15 | In article

Here is strategy how to solve 1-D quantum ising model problem exactly

$$
H=-\sum_i^N (\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x+g \sigma_i^z)
$$

$$
\sum_{i,j} J_{ij}[\sigma_i^z \sigma_j^z+\frac{1}{2}(\sigma_i^+\sigma_j^-+\sigma_i^-\sigma_j^+)]
$$

$$
H=-\sum_{i=1}^N (\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x+g \sigma_i^z)
$$

To get solution~!

the kinds of 1-D quantum ising model

  • Transverse Ising model

$$
H=-\sum_i^N (\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x+g \sigma_i^z)
$$

  • XY Ising model
    $$
    \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} J_{ij}[\sigma_{i}^+\sigma_{j}^-+\sigma_{i}^-\sigma_{j}^+]
    $$
  • Heisenberg model
    $$
    \sum_{i,j} J_{ij}[\sigma_i^z \sigma_j^z+\frac{1}{2}(\sigma_i^+\sigma_j^-+\sigma_i^-\sigma_j^+)]
    $$
    All the models are suitable for its special area and I want to discuss the first one in detail.

The limit and condition

as you see, it’s Hamiltonian is
$$
H=-\sum_{i=1}^N (\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x+g \sigma_i^z)
$$
and now I give the condition for the problem:

  • N is infinite
  • under the periodic boundary condition
  • the particle has only two state $\left| \uparrow \right\rangle$ and $\left| \downarrow \right\rangle$, which means that $\sigma^z \left| \uparrow \right\rangle =1$
  • ……………

Change Representation

what we want to do is implement some transformation and make Hamiltonian diagonalization. It is same process as the diagonalization for 1-D spring chain. But this situation is more difficult.

First of all, we imply Jordan - Wigner transformation.



The $\sigma^+$ and $\sigma^-$is the creative(annihilated) operator for spin-1/2 but it is not good enough. What I mean is ${\sigma_i^+,\sigma_i^-}=1$ and $[\sigma_i^+,\sigma_j^-]=0$ . So spins on different sites commute unlike fermions which anti-commute. We must remedy this before we can take the analogy very seriously. It is easy to solved by using Jordan-Wigner transformation which defines the new operator:
$$
c_j^+=e^{+i \pi \sum_{k=1}^{j-1}\sigma_k^+\sigma_k^-} \cdot \sigma_j^+ \\
c_j=e^{-i \pi \sum_{k=1}^{j-1}\sigma_k^+\sigma_k^-} \cdot \sigma_j^-\\
c_j^+ c_j =\sigma_j^+ \sigma_j^-
$$
and satisfied the appropriate fermionic commutation relations
$$
{c_i^+,c_i}=\delta_i^j,{c_i^+,c_j^+}=0,{c_i,c_j}=0
$$
the inverse transformation is
$$
\sigma_j^+=e^{-i \pi \sum_{k=1}^{j-1}c_k^+c_k} \cdot c_j^+ \\
\sigma_j^-=e^{+i \pi \sum_{k=1}^{j-1}c_k^+c_k} \cdot c_j\\
\sigma_j^z = 1 - 2 c_j^+ c_j
$$

$$
\sigma_j^z = 1 - 2 c_j^+ c_j , \sigma_i^x=(c_i+c_i^+)\prod_{j=1}^{i-1}(1-2c_j^+c_j),{c_i^+,c_i}=\delta_i^j,{c_i,c_j}=0
$$
Now we can present Hamiltonian using a good quasiparticle.
$$
H=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}f_{i,i+1}+f_{N,1}P-g\sum _{i=1}^N(c_ic_i^+ - c_i^+c_i) \\
f_{i,j}=c_i^+c_j-c_ic_j^+-c_ic_j+c_i^+c_j^+\\
P=\prod_{i=1}^N \sigma_i^z \quad \text{is parity operator}
$$



$$
\begin{align}
&\quad -\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} (\sigma_i^x \sigma_{i+1}^x+g \sigma_i^z)\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} [(c_i+c_i^+) (c_{i+1}+c_{i+1}^+)(1-2c_i^+c_i)\prod_{j=1}^{i-1}(1-2c_j^+c_j)^2+g \sigma_i^z]\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} [(c_i+c_i^+) (c_{i+1}+c_{i+1}^+)(1-2c_i^+c_i)+g \sigma_i^z]\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} [(c_i+c_i^+)(c_ic_i^+-c_i^+c_i) (c_{i+1}+c_{i+1}^+)+g \sigma_i^z]\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} [(c_i^+c_ic_i^+-c_ic_i^+c_i) (c_{i+1}+c_{i+1}^+)+g \sigma_i^z]\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}[(c_i^+-c_i) (c_{i+1}+c_{i+1}^+)+g \sigma_i^z]\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1} (c_i^+c_{i+1}+c_i^+c_{i+1}^+-c_ic_{i+1}^+-c_ic_{i+1}+g \sigma_i^z)\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}f_{i,i+1}-g\sum _{i=1}^{N-1}(c_ic_i^+ - c_i^+c_i) \\
\end{align}
$$

$$
\begin{align}
& \quad \sigma_N^x \sigma_{N+1}^x+g \sigma_N^z\\
&=\sigma_N^x \sigma_{1}^x+g \sigma_N^z\\
& = (c_N+c_N^+) (c_{1}+c_{1}^+)(1-2c_N^+c_N)(1-2c_N^+c_N)\prod_{j=1}^{N-1}(1-2c_j^+c_j)+g \sigma_N^z\\
& = (c_N+c_N^+) (c_{1}+c_{1}^+)(1-2c_N^+c_N)(1-2c_N^+c_N)P+g \sigma_N^z\\
& =- f_{N,1}P+g \sigma_N^z
\end{align}
$$

Now we focus in the c-particles representation, it is fermion. We know that for fermion system we can easily present a state like $ \left| 101 \cdots 010 \right\rangle$ and 1 means there is a particle in site i, 0 means none.

And the parity operator is easily determined by counting the c-particles number $N$. If $N$ is even so $P=1$ and rathan it is $P=-1$

You know the intact operator $P$ is a full rank operator which means $P \left| \phi \right\rangle=\pm \left| \phi \right\rangle,\forall \phi $ . To simplify discussion we can decompose $P$ into 2 orthogonality projectors $P^+$and$P^-$
$$
P^{\pm}=\frac{1}{2}(1\pm P)
$$
You can quickly verify they are actually projectors by
$$
P^+\left| \text{even motivate}\right\rangle=\left| \text{even motivate}\right\rangle,P^+\left| \text{odd motivate}\right\rangle=0\\
P^-\left| \text{odd motivate }\right\rangle=\left| \text{odd motivate}\right\rangle,P^-\left| \text{even motivate}\right\rangle=0
$$
And base on this decompose we can rewrite Hamiltonian:
$$
H=H^+P^++H^-P^-
$$
in this formule $H^+$ and $H^-$ have same shape
$$
H^{\pm}=-\sum_{i=1}^{N}f_{i,i+1}-g\sum _{i=1}^N(c_ic_i^+ - c_i^+c_i)
$$
but with different boundary condition:

  • $H^+$ are antiperiodic: $c_{N+1}=-c_1$
  • $H^-$ are periodic: $c_{N+1}=c_1$

Diagonalization

First of all, because it has some particular periodic property. We can apply Fourier transform to strip this symmetry.
$$
c_j=\frac{e^{i \pi /4}}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_k \mathfrak{c}_k e^{i k j}\quad\mathfrak{c}_k=\frac{e^{-i \pi /4}}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_j c_j e^{-i k j}
$$
Now we take the whole problem into 4 sub-problem and using another fermion operator to describe system and Hamiltonian

  • $H^+$ and the are totally even particle $\text{N is even}$

$$
H^+(\text{even N})=\sum_k \epsilon_k (2 \gamma_k^+\gamma_k-1),\epsilon_k=|(g-\cos(k),\sin(k))|\\
k=\pm \frac{\pi}{N},\pm \frac{3\pi}{N},\dots,\pm \frac{(N-1)\pi}{N} \quad \text{satisfy $c_{N+1}=-c_1$}
$$

  • $H^+$ and the are totally even particle $\text{N is odd}$

$$
H^+(\text{even N})=\sum_k \epsilon_k (2 \gamma_k^+\gamma_k-1)+(g+1)[\mathfrak{c}_{\pi}^+ ,\mathfrak{c}_{\pi}]\\
k=\pm \frac{\pi}{N},\pm \frac{3\pi}{N},\dots,\pm \frac{(N-2)\pi}{N} ,\pi\quad \text{satisfy $c_{N+1}=-c_1$}
$$

  • $H^-$ and the are totally even particle $\text{N is even}$

$$
H^+(\text{even N})=\sum_k \epsilon_k (2 \gamma_k^+\gamma_k-1)+(g-1)[\mathfrak{c}_0^+ ,\mathfrak{c}_0]
+(g+1)[\mathfrak{c}_{\pi}^+ ,\mathfrak{c}_{\pi}]\\
k=0,\pm \frac{2\pi}{N},\dots,\pm \frac{(N-2)\pi}{N},\pi \quad \text{satisfy $c_{N+1}=-c_1$}
$$

  • $H^-$ and the are totally even particle $\text{N is }odd$

$$
H^+(\text{even N})=\sum_k \epsilon_k (2 \gamma_k^+\gamma_k-1)+(g-1)[\mathfrak{c}_0^+ ,\mathfrak{c}_0]\\
k=0,\pm \frac{2\pi}{N},\dots,\pm \frac{(N-1)\pi}{N} \quad \text{satisfy $c_{N+1}=-c_1$}
$$



first of all
$$
\sum_{i=1}^N c_i^+c_{i+1}=\sum_{k} \mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}\\
\sum_{i=1}^N c_ic_{i+1}^+=\sum_{k} \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik}\\
\sum_{i=1}^N c_ic_{i+1}=\sum_{k} \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}\\
\sum_{i=1}^N c_i^+c_{i+1}^+=\sum_{k} \mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik}
$$

and

$$
\begin{align}
H^{\pm}
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N}f_{i,i+1}-g\sum _{i=1}^N(c_ic_i^+ - c_i^+c_i)\\
&=-\sum_{i=1}^{N}(c_i^+c_{i+1}-c_ic_{i+1}^+-c_ic_{i+1}+c_i^+c_{i+1}^+)-g\sum _{i=1}^N(c_ic_i^+ - c_i^+c_i)\\
&=-\sum_{k}[
\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik}
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}
+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik}
-g(
\mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+
-\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k)]\\
&=-\sum_{k}[
\frac{1}{2}(\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik})+
\frac{1}{2}(\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik})\\
&\qquad \qquad+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+ e^{-ik}
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k e^{ik}
-g(
\mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+
-\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k)]\\
&=\sum_{k\setminus {0,\pi}}[
(g-cos[k])(\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k^+)+sin[k] (\mathfrak{c}_k^+ \mathfrak{c}_k^+
- \mathfrak{c}_k \mathfrak{c}_k)\\
&\quad+\sum_{k=1}[(g-1)(\mathfrak{c}_0^+ \mathfrak{c}_0
- \mathfrak{c}_0 \mathfrak{c}_0^+)]\\
&\quad+\sum_{k=\pi}[(g+1)(\mathfrak{c}_{\pi}^+ \mathfrak{c}_{\pi}
- \mathfrak{c}_{\pi} \mathfrak{c}_{\pi}^+)]
\end{align}
$$
we now using the Bogolubov transformation
$$
\mathfrak{c}_k=\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\gamma_k - \sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\gamma_{-k}^+\\
\gamma_k=\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_k+ \sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_{-k}^+\\
(\cos(\theta_k),\sin(\theta_k))=Norm(g-\cos(k),\sin(k))
$$
it can be ensured that $\gamma_k$ is also a fermion operator that ${\gamma_k,\gamma_k^+}=\delta_i^j$ and${\gamma_k,\gamma_j}=0$

And then we can rewrite the Hamiltonian:
$$
H^{\pm}=\sum_k \epsilon_k (2 \gamma_k^+\gamma_k-1),\epsilon_k=|(g-\cos(k),\sin(k))|
$$

Now we can easily find the ground state in $\gamma$ representation by formula:
$$
\begin{align}
&\Longrightarrow\gamma_k\left| 0\right\rangle =0\\
&\Longrightarrow (\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_k+ \sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_{-k}^+)\left| 0\right\rangle =0
\end{align}
$$
if we just care the part which works we can assume $\left| 0\right\rangle = \otimes_k \left| 0\right\rangle_k$ which
$$
\left| 0\right\rangle_k
=\alpha \left| 0\right\rangle_k\left| 0\right\rangle_{-k}
+\beta \left| 0\right\rangle_k\left|1\right\rangle_{-k}
+\lambda \left| 1\right\rangle_k\left| 0\right\rangle_{-k}
+\tau \left| 1\right\rangle_k\left| 1\right\rangle_{-k}
$$
then you get
$$
\left| 0\right\rangle_k
=\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\left| 0\right\rangle_k\left| 0\right\rangle_{-k}
-\sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\left| 1\right\rangle_k\left| 1\right\rangle_{-k}
$$
Finally the ground state can be writen if $k\neq 0 \& \pi$:
$$
\left| 0\right\rangle
=\otimes_k [\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\left| 0\right\rangle_k\left| 0\right\rangle_{-k}
-\sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\left| 1\right\rangle_k\left| 1\right\rangle_{-k}]
$$

Example

Choose $N=4$ and then the $k$ for $H^+$ is ${\pm\frac{3 \pi}{4},\pm \frac{\pi}{4}}$

so the ground state is
$$
\begin{align}\left| 0\right\rangle
&=(\cos(\frac{\theta_{3\pi /4}}{2})\left| 0\right\rangle_{\frac{3 \pi}{4}}\left| 0\right\rangle_{-{\frac{3 \pi}{4}}}
-\sin(\frac{\theta_{3\pi /4}}{2})\left| 1\right\rangle_{\frac{3 \pi}{4}}\left| 1\right\rangle_{-{\frac{3 \pi}{4}}})\\
&\otimes(\cos(\frac{\theta_{\pi /4}}{2})\left| 0\right\rangle_{\frac{ \pi}{4}}\left| 0\right\rangle_{-{\frac{ \pi}{4}}}
-\sin(\frac{\theta_{\pi /4}}{2})\left| 1\right\rangle_{\frac{\pi}{4}}\left| 1\right\rangle_{-{\frac{ \pi}{4}}})\\
\end{align}
$$

ground state in site representation

we know the ground state in every system in theory. But the ground state is written in $\mathfrak{c}$ space, the next question is how to re-write it in site space.

what I mean is that take $N=4$ as the example, in site space we have $16$ natural bases
$$
\left| 0000\right\rangle,\left| 0001\right\rangle,\left| 0010\right\rangle,\left| 0011\right\rangle\\
\left| 0100\right\rangle,\left| 0101\right\rangle,\left| 0110\right\rangle,\left| 0111\right\rangle\\
\left| 1000\right\rangle,\left| 1001\right\rangle,\left| 1010\right\rangle,\left| 1011\right\rangle\\
\left| 1100\right\rangle,\left| 1101\right\rangle,\left| 1110\right\rangle,\left| 1110\right\rangle
$$
How to present the ground state using these $16$ bases?

Computational Way

In mathematics, we can regard the wave function $\left| 0\right\rangle$ as a vector and the operator as a matrix. We need to back to see where do we get ground state from. It is
$$
\gamma_k\left| 0\right\rangle =0,\forall k
$$
Now the question can be stated more mathematically :
$$
\left| 0\right\rangle \in \text{kernel of $\gamma_k$},\forall k
$$
or its equivalent form
$$
\left| 0\right\rangle \in \cap_k {ker(\gamma_k)}
$$
Luckily, we can easily present $\gamma_k$ in site presentation using
$$
\gamma_k=\cos(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_k+ \sin(\frac{\theta_k}{2})\mathfrak{c}_{-k}^+\\
\mathfrak{c}_k=\frac{e^{-i \pi /4}}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_j c_j e^{-i k j}\\
c_j=e^{-i \pi \sum_{k=1}^{j-1}\sigma_k^+\sigma_k^-} \cdot \sigma_j^-=\prod_{i=1}^{j-1}(-\sigma_i^z)\sigma_j^-
$$

example

$$
c_j=\prod_{i=1}^{j-1}(-\sigma_i^z)\sigma_j^-
$$
it means that

c_3\left| 0101\right\rangle=0 \left| 0111\right\rangle,c_4\left| 0101\right\rangle=- \left| 0100\right\rangle
$$
the phase depends on the number of $1$ before the site, that is all!

Now it can be solved in computer and using a little trick I can make computational problem easier.

This trick is about how to compute the intersection of all the $\gamma_k$’s kernel. Just compute the big block matrix’s kernel.
$$
\left(\begin{array}{c} \gamma _1 \ \gamma _2 \ \cdots \ \gamma _{k_{n-1}} \ \gamma _{k_n} \\\end{array}\right)_{(k_n\cdot2^N) \times 2^N}
\cdot \qquad
\left(\begin{array}{c} X _1 \ X _2 \ \cdots \ X_{n-1} \ X _n \\\end{array}\right)_{2^N \times1}
=
\left(\begin{array}{c}0 \\0 \ \cdots \\0 \\0 \\\end{array}\right)_{2^N \times1}
$$

Algebric way

This solution is based on the fact that vacuum state is unique and can be presented in site space and $\mathfrak{c}$ space simultaneously. Take $N=4$ as example
$$
\left| vac \right\rangle=\left| 0000\right\rangle_{site}=\left| 0000\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}
$$
In physics, $\left| vac\right\rangle$ means there are no excited particle

so we can get $\left| 0000\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}$from
$$
\begin{align}
\left| 0001\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}
&=\mathfrak{c}_4^+ \left| 0000\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}\\
&=\frac{e^{-i \pi /4}}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_j c_j e^{-i 4 j}\left| 0000\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}\\
&=\frac{e^{-i \pi /4}}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_j c_j e^{-i 4 j}\left| 0000\right\rangle_{site}\\
\end{align}
$$
Now, we can use $\left| 0000\right\rangle_{site},\left| 0001\right\rangle_{site},\left| 0010\right\rangle_{site},\left| 0011\right\rangle_{site} \dots \left| 1111\right\rangle_{site}$ to represent any $\left|\varphi\right\rangle_{\mathfrak{c}-space}$

However, for computing, this way is not very good for a universal algorithm.

Concurrence

Even for small N, we can still get many interesting understandings.

The first one is QPT–quantum phase transition~! for concurrence.

Before we start discussing, we need to get to figure out ‘what is concurrence’.

Quantum entanglement has rightly been the subject of much study in recent years as a potential resource for communication and information processing. As with other resources such as free energy and information, one would like to have a quantitative theory of entanglement giving specific rules about how it can and cannot be manipulated; indeed, such a theory has begun to be developed. The first step in building the theory has been to quantify entanglement itself.

The most famous way is called concurrence. Underline! Concurrence can only directly measure two system entanglement!

we can define any state’s concurrence by its density matrix $\rho_{AB}$
$$
\tilde{\rho} _{\text{AB}}=(\sigma _y\otimes \sigma _y) \rho _{\text{AB}} (\sigma _y\otimes \sigma _y)
$$

$$
\sigma _y=\left(
\begin{array}{cc}
0 & -i \\
i & 0 \\
\end{array}
\right)
$$

then we put $\rho_{AB}$and $\tilde{\rho}_{AB}$ together, consider the eigenvalue of matrix $\rho_{AB}\tilde{\rho}_{AB}$
$$
\lambda _1 ,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\lambda_4 =eign(\rho_{AB}\tilde{\rho}_{AB})
$$

$$
\lambda _1> \lambda_2>\lambda_3>\lambda_4
$$

and the concurrence is be defined follow
$$
C_{AB}=\max { \sqrt{\lambda _1} - \sqrt{\lambda_2}-\sqrt{\lambda_3}-\sqrt{\lambda_4} ,0 }
$$
we can prove if $\rho$ is a pure state we can easily get
$$
C_{AB}=2 \sqrt{\det \rho_A}
$$

$$
\rho_A = Tr_B \rho_{AB}
$$

you can find more detail in the reference Distributed entanglement Valerie Coffman
